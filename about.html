Vision

My vision on design

Traditionally, lighting designers have created luminaires with light as a property of the product. Iconic lamps from, say, Le Klint or Serge Mouille are designed with aesthetic quality of the product as a whole in mind. The lamps were supposed to look good, and emit pleasant lighting, but did not serve any greater purpose. The type of light and the effect it had on the consumer was seen as a property of the product. Some designers, such as David Wahl (Ikea), whose Pendant Luminaire takes after a design from Poul Henningsen, introduced more kinetic principles in their designs, giving users more control over how their lighting would be perceived. Here, light was used as a backbone for interactivity; the lamp was changed, but not the light itself. A popular example of light as interactive tool is Philips Hue (Signify), which allows users to adapt the intensity and colour of their lighting to suit their needs. This illustrates the state of lighting today, where light is used as an adaptive tool to change how it is perceived.

I wish to take the transition further, and influence how lighting design will develop in the future. To take full advantage of the wide potential of light in design, we need to better understand the dynamic relationship between people and light. I foresee that light can serve as a complement to, or an extension of people, such that it is possible to interact with light in a way that is both rich and intuitive.

Designers can reshape the way we interact with light. They can serve as intermediary between people and technology, use creative ways to further the transition to interactive lighting, and design lighting products that adhere to demands and are desirable and beautiful in their use. This is what I would like to focus on as an Industrial Designer; I want to push the boundaries of what is possible with light, and the way we live with it.

Light is often utilised in artistic expression. Artists use light to create magical experiences and atmospheres, or convey certain feelings or messages. Olafur Eliasson, to name one, is known for creating impressive installations with light as a driving factor. Following this trend, I will continue to use light not as a static backdrop to our tasks, but rather as an experiential agent that is dynamic, interactive and intelligent.

My Professional Identity

My interest in Lighting Design reflects this as well; I find light a fascinating subject because it involves science, engineering and research (the properties of light, effects on the mind and body, colour theory, etc.) and provides a broad and exciting canvas in terms of artistry and aesthetics.

As a Designer, I always try to find a balance: between art and tech, and between abstract and concrete. I am able to view projects from an observant viewpoint, conceptualising the overall systems and ideas as an abstract entity. But I can also be detail-oriented, focussing on the direct interaction between the product and a person. In a professional setting, I am empathic, and I can understand different visions from clients, stakeholders and myself, and combine them into a single unified concept that is both up to expectation, as well as surprising and new. E.g. during my internship at VANTOT, when I was tasked with designing facade branding for a warehouse developer, and designed a novel concept that plays with sunlight and perspective, based on the indentity of the brand.

I can work well on my own and am adept at problem-solving, sometimes using unconventional methods when other options are unavailable. I don't give up easily, and will always give a project my all, often going above and beyond when it comes to things like styling, video editing, rendering, etc. Over the years, I have also learned to become better at collaborating. Where I used to struggle with finding my voice or being confident enough to communicate my ideas, I have become much more sure of myself and can now lead the way in group discussions or push other team members to act.

Overall, I feel I have gained a well-rounded set of skills, which allows me to contribute to several phases of a design process: brainstorming and conceptualising, prototyping, hardware and software development, as well as presenting and showcasing. I value creativity in designs, and will often try to question the status quo of things. Oftentimes I will attempt to find ways to go against the norm and try something new, as I believe that herein lies the strength of an Industrial Designer. This was an important aspect of my Final Bachelor Project, CHIARO, as it was an exploration of a novel way of lighting control.
