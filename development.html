Development

During my 4 years of studying Industrial Design, I have continuously been developing as a designer. Since my first year in 2020, I have discovered my passion for lighting, explored what kind of designer I am and wish to be, and been involved in a wide variety of projects, both in the curriculum and outside of it. In the following section, I will walk through a selection of the most influential activities in my studies, reflecting on the impact they have had on my personal development.

B1 - 2020

Creative Programming

One of my main reasons for switching to Industrial Design from Applied Physics was the prospect of doing something technical yet creative. One of the first courses I did - Creative Programming - allowed me to do exactly that. I already had some coding experience, but none yet in Arduino and Processing. I was able to quickly pick this up, which opened up a realm of possibilities for me - now I could create interactive prototypes. For the challenges, I programmed a piece of code that could randomly generate an art piece resembling a flower, and a ping pong game where the ball is substituted for a laser. The course ended up cementing my goal of combining the artistic with the technical in my design career.

B1.2 - 2021

Physie

I did my first major Design project - Project 1 - within the theme of playful learning. Physie is a playful modular tool designed to help with getting an intuitive understanding for physics. This project was my first foray into user-centered design, after only having had some theoretical education in the User Centered Design course. Back then, I reflected on how nervous I was to do in-person user testing, as this was still completely new to me (the pandemic didn't quite help, either). Looking back, I realise how much I have changed in the way I am able to present myself professionally. I am now much more confident during interviews and other user tests.

I furthermore discovered that I have a knack for finding creative solutions to problems, especially when it comes to electronics and software. For example, we needed a way to track a ball in 3D space, measuring its position and velocity. This could be done with an accelerometer and very advanced mathematics, but I figured this was way beyond our scope of capabilites. Instead, using my then limited knowledge of electronics, I implemented an array of hall sensors, that could track the magnet inside of the ball. This method of problem-solving has since then become a vital part of my professional identity.

USE - 2020-2021

Secret Life of Light

I chose The Secret Life of Light as my USE Learning Line. It initially sparked my interest due to being a technical, yet also creative course. Little did I know, that this choice would end up being the most impactful in my career development. The learning line introduced me to lighting science and terminology, new research methods, and had me conduct multiple projects that explored various facets of lighting design: an outdoor lighting design proposal for District E (Redesign of Eindhoven Central Station area), a photometric analysis of my apartment, and ultimately a Wayfinding concept for GLOW Eindhoven. This project, titled 'Glass Your Way', was a play on the four distinct visitor personality types. An illuminated glass would guide each visitor type along a custom route through the festival, tailored to their needs and desires.

The learning line as a whole was really enjoyable to me, and while I did not realise it at the time, it ignited my passion for lighting as a designer. At the end, I was presented with the opportunity to continue working on the GLOW wayfinding project as an extracurricular activity, which I naturally wholeheartedly agreed to.

Wayfinding

I continued the wayfinding project with a new team, and this was my first large-scale extracurricular activity. Although it was not the first project I did within a multi-disciplinary team, it was a collaboration that I enjoyed much more than, say, Engineering Design. The great challenge of the project was attributed to very limited time and budget. We needed to cover the entire TU/e campus with our concept, so the cost and production time per unit had to be minimised. I regret that I did not have a large contribution to the concept development - back then, I was rather overwhelmed by the entire ordeal, and felt quite inexperienced as I was one of the youngest in the group. Nevertheless, I tried to contribute wherever I could, and I gave it my all when I needed to.

In the end, I could feel a little proud of what we achieved as a group in such short time, and we even won the Crowe talent jury award for our creativity and perseverance. It made me excited to do more of these kind of projects, and I set out that I would do better every time as I continued to learn and be more confident in my abilities.

B2.2 - 2022

Zymotie

At the time of Project 3, I was still developing and exploring my identity and vision. After following the course Design for Debate, which focused on posthumanism, I wanted to further explore this theme. Zymotie came about as a result, which attempted to redefine the bond between humans and bacteria by providing insight into the process of fermentation. A dynamically animated artifact would communicate the states and desires of the bacteria present during the fermentation of yoghurt. The project challenged my empathic ability as designer. We needed to design for both human and bacteria, and so we used a couple of methods to familiarise ourselves with both entities. We visited a museum of micro-organisms and used roleplaying methods in our design process. I learned to expand my worldview and to be more empathetic as a designer, but I did end up not pursuing post-humanism any further in my vision.

I was mostly behind the execution of the prototype, and although I was able to get it working, I was not fully satisfied with the build quality and aesthetics of it. This led to my becoming more aware of the aesthetics and build quality in later prototypes.

Team IGNITE - Paper Trails

The continuation of the Wayfinding project was taken up by the then reforming Team IGNITE. With most of the members having left the team after their GLOW 2021 project, the student team was in the midst of redefining their structure and vision. I rejoined the project not long after, which meant that I was able to be part of this new direction for IGNITE, and could witness its development and growth. Being a member of such a creative and tight-knit community has meant a lot for my personal as well as professional development, too. This project, rebranded to Paper Trails, especially challenged my team-working abilities. Our team encouraged feedback-giving and open discussions, which led me to become more confident in myself - to realise that my opinion matters, too, and that I am allowed to share my thoughts. As a designer, I needed to learn not to be hesitant to share my ideas, even if they weren't very good. Any input at all is better than none, and will often lead to other, better concepts or meaningful discussions.

I was mostly responsible for programming, animation, and visuals. By doing so, I became a lot more competent in image rendering, and photoshopping, which was helpful for convincing other parties of our lighting concepts at an early stage of the process. Good communication is key in Design, and clear visuals that convey not just form, but atmosphere and feelings as well, are therefore powerful tools.

Another valuable lesson I learned was how to cooperate with 3rd parties when we got the opportunity to present our work at Eindhoven Airport for a few months. I needed to collaborate with the staff from the airport as well as with Hypar, who were present at the same time.

B3.1 - 2023

Internship Studio VANTOT

Doing an intenship at a lighting design studio was definitely one of my most valuable experiences. I discovered that I am good at combining the visions of myself, the studio I work for, as well as the client, into a unique design concept that satisfies all parties. I also adapted my workflow with suggestions from my internship coaches, such as using better drawing technques for lighting concepts. They also introduced me to parametric design. Using blender, I was able to develop my own parametric design tools, which enabled me to conduct a Reflective Transformative Design Process in a unique and novel way.

Working in a small business gave me some much needed insights into Business and Entrepreneurship. I got to participate in meetings with clients, and found out how a back-and-forth between designers, clients and partners happens in practice. By discussing my design concepts with VANTOT's technical partner, who produce custom PCB's, I was able to make informed decisions about the future steps of the project and how to best develop my ideas in a technically feasible way.

The studio also gave me lots of practical insights of Design that I find are missing from the Bachelor. I got to work in their workshop, learned how to weld, used professional electronic parts, and I could even join them when they installed their light sculpture at a large company. Looking back, I realise that since my time at VANTOT I have become much more practical design-oriented, rather than research-oriented as I had largely been before.

Extracurricular - 2022-2023

Team IGNITE - GLOWBLE

My ultimate project as member of Team IGNITE is GLOWBLE, a large-scale interactive light artwork. This was one of the greatest challenges I have partaken in during my bachelor, and it took about a year to develop. This project was my gateway to industry-standard technologies and protocols that are relevant in the Lighting Design Branche. I used the software TouchDesigner to develop the interactive visuals and map those onto our custom-built lighting setup. Our lights were controlled using the DMX protocol, a standard protocol commonly used in the entertainment branche. Being able to gain experience in such professional technologies is a huge advantage for my later career as lighting designer.

The project furthermore included a novel technology in the form of acoustic cameras, provided by Sorama. I believe that as a designer, one of my objectives is to introduce technological innovations to the public in accessible ways. The fact that I was able to translate the data from the sound cameras into an interactive artwork signifies that I have developed my technical and creative skills enough to fulfill this. Being actively involved in the production and build-up of the large installation has also provided me with lots of practical experience that will surely come in handy when I create more lighting installations in the future.

Finally, Project GLOWBLE is one of the first team-work environments where I truly felt comfortable and confident. I allowed myself to be present, and to just be myself without fear of judgement. It ressured me that I am capable, and that often the only one holding me back had been myself. Going forward, I believe this experience will continue to be a significant contribution to my development as a confident and capable designer. Winning another award with this - the GLOW Talent public vote award - was the cherry on top of a thrilling and educational time. We even got the opportunity to showcase our work beyond GLOW at Dagcollege, an indoor techno festival.

Final Bachelor Project - 2024

CHIARO

The culmination of all my development over the four years is CHIARO - a hybrid lighting control device that explores a new way of interacting with the light in our home. As I had discovered my desire to become a lighting designer, I naturally wanted to relate my Final Bachelor Project to the theme of lighting. Throughout the Bachelor I had indulged myself with all kinds of lighting design, from light art installations to interior lighting, but I lacked some experience still in Product Design, so I knew for certain that I wanted to design a smart lighting product. The project truly came forth out of past experiences: Given that the FBP is an individual project, I had to rely only on myself, which I wouldn't have been able to do had I not gained as much confidence as I have; I put a lot of effort in the technical and aesthetic aspects of the design, fuelled by the results from e.g. Zymotie; I was able to implement TouchDesigner, to program complex behaviour more easily, which I learned during GLOWBLE; I used certain analysis tools from Business and Entrepreneurship courses to evaluate the product-market fit of the concept in relation to the squad persona's; And I was able to conduct insightful user tests thanks to years of developing my social and empathic skills.

Ultimately I feel I can be proud of what I have achieved, and when reflecting on my overall development over the years I can see I have definitely changed much since I first started the Bachelor in 2020.

Overall Development

Creativity and Aesthetics

I am able to conduct an iterative process of design, using various methods and tools that I have learned. I can view a project from different perspectives, using either a conceptual, technical lens or an artistic one, focusing on details and aesthetics. Light lends itself well to this kind of thinking, as it blends the scientific with the artistic. I often work from a certain vision of a project – a rough estimation of what I feel a project should convey – but I can balance this with knowledge from research, user testing and professional advice. During my time at Team IGNITE we regularly met up with experts like Philip Ross to get a professional’s perspective on our projects, for instance.

From courses like Aesthetics of Interaction I have gained a critical understanding of interaction and can use methods like interaction relabelling or the interaction frogger framework to inform design decisions. For example, the designs of the CHIARO handheld and charging stand are based on feedforward for the actions of respectively grasping and picking up the handheld.

I have learned to use visual techniques like sketching, modelling and rendering to convey not only forms, but ideas and emotions as well. The course Exploratory Making has taught me to use physical modelling as another explorative tool. I applied this in my FBP when translating the design requirements resulting from the wizard-of-oz user test into physical forms that afforded these required interactions. Additionally, I can creatively make use of tools that are available to me to advance the reflective transformative design process, like when I used the Geometry Nodes tool in Blender to program parametric design tools, allowing me to quickly evaluate and explore variations of a concept.

User and Society

Since I started out as a rather shy individual, this was the Expertise Area I felt I needed to develop most to become a well-rounded designer. Over the years, I have become more confident and less afraid to approach people. I have learned to be more emphatic, and to break free from my own perspective: learning to do e.g. an explorative user test has turned out incredibly helpful, especially regarding my FBP. Without the Wizard-of-Oz user test I did I would likely have been stuck on an inadequate concept.

One course in particular encouraged design as a form of breaking from the societal mould – Design for Debate taught me to continuously question the status quo and adopt various perspectives when designing. I used the insights from this course to develop my vision of innovation in lighting.

Light is as much a physical, as it is a psychological phenomenon. In courses like Advancing Light for human functioning and Physics of light and lighting design, I have delved into the human aspect of light – how it affects our wellbeing, perception and emotions. It has given a basic understanding of how I should design light to be appropriate for people, yet I also came to acknowledge the differences and nuances between individuals. Using qualitative research methods like the co-constructing stories method or wizard-of-oz method, I am able to evaluate the unique perspectives of the individual. Additionally, I can conduct quantitative research to acquire a more generalised conception of what are appropriate interventions.

Technology and Realization

Throughout my studies I have experimented with a wide variety of electronics, ranging from micro (microcontrollers in e.g. CHIARO) to macro (large-scale light artwork like GLOWBLE). I have learned to implement sensors and actuators and to use them in unconventional ways when needed. For instance, when I used the hall sensors to track the position, velocity and acceleration of Physie. I can also integrate professional protocols and frameworks into custom-made solutions. For example: using TouchDesigner to translate data from novel technology (acoustic camera) into visuals over DMX. Or connecting our project to the audio system of a techno festival to make GLOWBLE sound reactive.

I am furthermore able to make informed design decisions based on technical datasheets, professional advice, like when I was developing a new product with VANTOT and their technical partner BHC.

In the course Creative Mechanical Engineering – basic, I learned theories on Mechanics and manufacturing processes. I also learned volumetric 3D modelling and how to analyse models on feasibility using Finite Element Analysis. Now, I tend to use 3D modelling techniques during creative brainstorming phases of a project, as it helps me with visualising complex geometries and allows me to further manipulate a design concept in 3D space. This has helped me make sense of my thoughts more than any other visualising technique I used before, advancing not only my technical abilities, but my skill in creativity and aesthetics as well.

Business and Entrepreneurship

I am able to use various entrepreneurial methods and analysis tools taught in courses like Introduction to Technology Entrepreneurship and Design Innovation Methods, such as the Business Model Canvas, Customer Journey Mapping, MVPs, Problem-market fit canvas, and more. Being part of an Innovation Space student team, I also got to experience first-hand what it would be like to be a designer in a startup. Although I wasn’t part of the board who managed things like IP and finance, we all did receive some guidance and workshops from IS regarding these topics, so that I know what to expect when I enter the workforce later.

During my internship I experienced design within a professional environment, dealing with clients, partners, manufacturers, and the studio itself. I learned how these stakeholders relate to each other, how a design process constitutes a constant back-and-forth between the parties, and I learned how to communicate with these stakeholders in the appropriate way. While working at VANTOT, I have also learned to contextualise design concepts in a financial environment. I often needed to adapt my concepts or change technical implementations based on financial considerations or remarks from the clients. It has made me realise that design is always going to be bound by limitations, but that there is often room for pushing the boundaries. For example, our client Stellar already had an idea of what they wanted us to design, but I still managed to surprise them with a concept that adhered to the brief yet put an interesting twist on it.

Math, Data and Computing

I am able to transform abstract data into meaningful applications in a variety of ways. For example, GLOWBLE makes use of acoustic camera’s that provide data on the amplitude and source location of sounds, outputting an array of decibels. By strategically placing three of these camera around the artwork and cleverly mapping the data I was able to transform the data stream into a 360 degree interactive experience that reacts dynamically to the sounds you make. The technology was used to constitute a reflection on the relationship between humans and technological systems, and to encourage an active exploration of this very relationship.

I have often found my past experience with Physics to be quite valuable, as I am adept at using mathematics and calculus to solve complex problems or handle data efficiently. This allows me to design intricate behaviour for objects and incorporate advanced interaction principles. The interaction of CHIARO may seem simple on surface-value, but is actually rather complex, as it needs to account for multiple states (docked or handheld), gyroscopic and magnetic readings to determine pointing direction, a multipurpose distance sensor, as well as communication with the smart lights. Having this experience with maths and programming has allowed me to design advanced interaction with profound depth, so that I can create meaningful experiences.
